What is Common Cold

Common cold is a non-serious disease caused by several viruses. Common cold viruses The following viruses cause a cold: Rhinovirus: causes 10% - 40% of cases of cold RSV - Respiratory syncytial virus: This type of virus causes 20% of the disease. Coronavirus: Coronavirus causes 10% of the common cold. In addition, influenza viruses (Influenza) - Adenovirus and Parainfluenza - cause symptoms similar to the common cold, but they are usually accompanied by severe pneumonia or a more severe disease. The Echovirus and Coxsackie virus also cause symptoms similar to the common cold, but infection with these viruses does not usually lead to the appearance of symptoms characteristic of the common cold, or it is accompanied by the appearance of a fever (high temperature) only. It is difficult to identify and distinguish the virus causing it on the basis of symptoms only, although the common parasite affects children usually, while RSV causes a mild cold. Most respiratory viruses that cause the common cold can lead to the disease returning and re-emerging when exposed to the virus again, but the disease is less severe in this case. Nasal and parasitic influenza viruses cause colds in the fall and late spring, while adenoviruses, wreathed viruses and respiratory syncytes cause illness in the winter and spring. The Ikoyi and Koksakki viruses are the ones that cause colds in the summer.

Transmission of the virus from a sick person to a healthy one

The common cold viruses are transmitted by direct touch or by spray. The direct transmission of the virus is considered the most effective mechanism for nasal viruses. The transmission of the virus from one person to another depends on the amount of time the person spends next to the sick person and the amount of viruses the patient has. Touch transfer is the primary method for transmitting infection. The virus is transmitted in contaminated saliva secretions from the patient to another person, through the hands and from there to the nose and eyes. The virus can live for two hours on the skin and has been found in 40% - 90% of the common cold patients. Nasal viruses are also transmitted by droplets (small droplets of saliva emitted into the air during exhalation, coughing and sneezing). The respiratory syncytial virus is a very contagious virus, and infection with this virus is usually caused by droplets (sneezing), although the virus persists for about an hour at a temperature of 37 Â° C. In 90% of patients who have symptoms, the virus is not found in saliva, which leads to the belief that the virus is not transmitted through kissing

Symptoms of Common Cold

The incubation time for the virus ranges from 24 to 72 hours. Symptoms vary from person to person and include: Runny nose and mucous congestion in the nose Stuffy nose Sneezing Sore throat. Often, sore throats disappear quickly, while gonorrhea and congestion remain the primary source of complaint in patients, especially on the second and third days after the onset of the disease. On the fourth and fifth days, coughing becomes the first inconvenience, while other symptoms subside. The common cold usually lasts between 3-7 days, although it lasts for about two more additional weeks in about a quarter of patients. The common cold may be more difficult and severe in children, in people with chronic diseases, in people with impaired immune system function and in those who suffer from malnutrition.

Complications of the common cold

Possible complications of a cold include: Sinusitis: caused by germs, which occurs in 0.5% - 2.5% of adults, after a cold. Similar inflammation caused by viruses is more prevalent, and computerized imaging tests have shown that this inflammation appears in 39% of people with a cold, after a week. Distinctive symptoms: purulent runny headache, persistent fever. Inflammation of the lungs: mainly due to infection with respiratory syncytial virus. Asthma worsening: about 40% of asthma attacks are caused by a cold.

Treating a cold

In general, the treatment of colds is aimed at alleviating the signs and symptoms of the disease. Cold therapy includes anti-histamines that can relieve cough and runny nose, cough suppressing medications, and medications to relieve mucous congestion in the nose (usually nasal drops). It has been shown in a number of studies that the treatment of colds with zinc (Vitamin C) and herbal medicine can be useful and effective in treating

symptoms of the common cold

There is no need to be treated with antibiotics, as it was not proven effective in treating the common cold, unless during the cold period a bacterial infection / infection occurred, it can be treated with antibiotics. It has recently proven the efficacy of anti-viral treatment, whether combined with or without anti-inflammatory drugs, as it can relieve the patient and even shorten the disease.
