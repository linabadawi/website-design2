What is Allergie

Allergie / Allergy is an immune system reaction to substances unfamiliar to it, such as pollen grains, poison from bee stings or animal dander. The immune system produces a protein called antibody (or: antibody - Antibody .IgE). These antibodies protect the body from unwanted foreign bodies, which may invade the body and cause disease or infection. When a person suffers from allergic reactions to a specific substance, his immune system produces antibodies that identify the allergen (the allergen, which causes allergen) and treats it as a harmful element, although it is not, which leads to the release of histamine and substances. Others cause allergy symptoms. Allergic reactions can cause various symptoms that may appear on the skin, in the sinuses, in the respiratory airways and in the digestive system. The severity of the sensitivity and the degree of severity of the sensitivity differ from one person to another, as the sensitivity can range from slight stimulation to anaphylaxis, an emergency that may cause death. The allergy cannot be fully cured, but there are some treatments that help relieve allergy symptoms.

Symptoms of Allergie

Symptoms of allergies differ in the same allergy, from one person to another. Symptoms of allergies may appear in the respiratory airways, in the sinuses and nasal passages, in the skin and in the digestive system. In most cases, the allergic reaction is a nuisance and distress, but the symptoms of allergies are mild. As for reactions in case of acute allergies, they may be more dangerous, because they may affect many organs and systems in the body. For more severe allergies, an allergic reaction can cause death. This phenomenon is known as anaphylaxis - a response to severe allergies with hypersensitivity to proteins or other substances that lead to severe respiratory and circulatory / vascular disorders. Hay fever (also called hay fever), also called allergic rhinitis, can cause: Nasal congestion, itching Runny nose Watery or swelling in the eyes (conjunctivitis - Conjunctivitis). Atopic Dermatitis, also known as Eczema, may cause: Itchy skin Skin redness Skin scaling Food Allergy may cause: Feeling pinched in the mouth Swelling of the lips, tongue, face or throat Urticaria (Urticaria) Anaphylaxis. Allergy to insect stings may cause: Swelling in a wide area (Edema) around the site of the sting Itching or urticaria throughout the body Coughing, chest contractions, crackling and wheezing, or Shortness of breath Anaphylaxis. Drug Allergy may cause: Urticaria Itching Skin rash Facial swelling Gobbling and wheezing at the time Anaphylaxis. Anaphylaxis Some types of allergies, including food allergies and insect stings, may cause a dangerous phenomenon known as anaphylaxis. This allergy may end in death and requires immediate medical treatment. This sensitivity affects many parts of the body and its systems and may cause a shock (Shock). Signs and symptoms of anaphylaxis include: Syncope (or: unconsciousness) Dizziness Severe shortness of breath Rapid and weak flickering Skin rash Nausea and vomiting (Emesis / Vomiting) Airway respiratory distention, which may stop breathing completely. In the event of an acute allergic reaction (anaphylaxis), urgent medical treatment should be directed immediately. If an injection of adrenaline (also called: Epinephrine) is available for self-injection, it should be used immediately. Even if the symptoms improve after the adrenaline injection, however, you should go to the emergency department to ensure that the symptoms do not reappear, once the injection is done.

Causes and risk factors for Allergie

An allergy occurs due to a fault in the immune system when it identifies a non-harmful substance as a dangerous invasive force. In this case, the immune system produces antibodies that are constantly on standby and prepared to fight the allergen. When the body is again being allergenic, the antibodies stimulate the release of a number of immune system chemicals, such as histamine, that cause allergic symptoms. Common allergens include: Airborne allergens, such as: pollen grains, animal dander, dust mites and mold. Some foods, especially nuts, nuts, wheat, soybeans, fish, shellfish, eggs and milk. Poison from insects, such as bee stings or wasps stings. Some medicines, especially penicillin or some penicillin-based antibiotics (Penicillin). Natural rubber (milky juice secreted by some plants - Latex) or other substances whose contact can provoke allergic reactions in the skin. The risk of allergies increases for people belonging to one of the following groups: They have a Family History of Asthma. The risk of developing allergies increases among relatives of patients who suffer from asthma or allergies, such as: hay fever, urticaria or eczema. Children - Although allergies may appear at any age, children are more susceptible than adults to allergies. Sometimes the sensitivity in children increases as they get older. However, in many cases, the allergy disappears and then returns later in the future. Asthma patients or people with allergies - the likelihood of developing allergies is greater for asthma patients, and people who suffer from a specific type of allergy are at risk of having an allergy to other substances, too.

Complications of Allergie

Allergies increase your risk of exposure to other health problems, including: Anaphylaxis - a person who suffers from acute allergic reactions is more likely to develop anaphylaxis, which is a reaction that results from the characteristics of an allergy. Anaphylaxis is mainly associated with nutritional sensitivity and sensitivity to insect toxins. Other allergy - People with one type of allergy are more likely to develop other types of allergies. Asthma - People with asthma - a reaction by the immune system that affects the airways and breathing - is more likely to develop allergies. Asthma often occurs as a result of exposure to a allergen in the environment (asthma caused by allergies). Atopic dermatitis (eczema), sinusitis, and ear or lung infections - the risk of developing these diseases increases in people with hay fever (hay fever), from animal allergy or from mold allergy. Fungal infections in the sinuses or lungs - the risk of these two diseases, known as fungal sinusitis and Aspergillosis, increases in people who are allergic to mold.
