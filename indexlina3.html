What is epilepsy

Epilepsy is a defect in the transmission of electrical badges within the brain. Although the common belief is that epilepsy always causes a fit of involuntary movement and loss of consciousness, in reality, epilepsy appears in very varied forms. Cases in which signs known as seizures appear are often implicit, provoking strange feelings, increased sensitivity, and irregular behavior. Some people with epilepsy stare into space for a while when they have a seizure, while others suffer from severe seizures and cramps. One in every 100 people in the United States suffered, during his lifetime, an inexplicable epileptic seizure. However, a one-time epileptic seizure does not constitute an indication of the presence of epilepsy. Many people, children who suffer a lot from fever, for example, may have a one-time epileptic seizure. But if the situation recurs and a person suffers from two epileptic seizures, the probability of experiencing another third seizure rises very seriously. At least two epileptic seizures are needed in order to diagnose epilepsy. Epilepsy, in general, appears in childhood or in adults over 65 years of age. However, epilepsy may appear at any age. Treating the correct and appropriate epilepsy can help prevent epileptic seizures, or at least reduce the frequency and severity of their occurrence. Indeed, many children and boys with epilepsy recover and recover from adulthood.

Symptoms of epilepsy

Epilepsy is caused by erratic brain cell activity, so epileptic seizures may cause harm to any body work that is coordinated by the brain. An seizure may cause temporary confusion, complete loss of consciousness, staring in space or involuntary shivering movements in the hands and feet. The signs and manifestations of epileptic seizures vary according to their type. In most cases, if a person suffers from frequent epileptic seizures, he tends to develop the same signs and symptoms in each seizure, so that the signs accompanying the seizure become the same from one seizure to another. However, there are other patients who suffer from different types of seizures, the signs and symptoms of which vary from time to time. Generally, doctors tend to classify seizures as partial or general, according to the picture in which the irregular activity in the brain has started. If the seizure appears as a result of irregular activity in only one part of the brain, this is a Partial seizure or Focal seizure. The seizure, during which there is an irregular activity in all parts of the brain, is called a general seizure. In some cases, the seizure may start in some part of the brain and then transfer to all parts of the brain.

Causes and risk factors for epilepsy

Certain types of epilepsy can be attributed to a defect in the genes responsible for the way the brain cells communicate with each other, but it is well known that only rare types of the disease are caused by certain impaired genes. Moreover, it appears that the disorder of any group of hundreds of genes can be a central factor in the emergence and development of epilepsy. Although it appears that certain types of epilepsy are transmitted from one generation to another, the genetic genetic factors constitute only one of the factors that can cause the emergence of epilepsy, and may be due to the fact that some people tend to be affected, more Others, by environmental factors that can lead to seizures. An epileptic seizure may occur, in many cases, as a result of an accident, illness or medical shock, for example a stroke that causes brain damage or prevents oxygen from reaching the brain. In rare cases, the cause of epilepsy may be a tumor in the brain. In all cases, not all epileptic seizures are caused by one specific factor that can be diagnosed and identified. This applies to about half of people with epilepsy. Among the risk factors: Family history Head injuries Stroke or other diseases of the blood vessels Contaminated brain diseases, such as meningitis and persistent convulsions and tremors during childhood, due to high fever and fever.

Epilepsy complications

If a person fails and stumbles the moment he develops an epileptic seizure, he may receive a blow to his head, and a person with an epileptic attack is likely to be drowned while swimming or bathing in the bathtub. An epileptic seizure that leads to loss of consciousness or loss of control may be very serious if it occurs while driving or while operating machinery. Medicines designed to suppress seizures may cause drowsiness, which may limit the ability to drive. In many US states, there are restrictions in driving licenses, depending on the ability of the person with epilepsy to suppress seizures. Epileptic seizures in a pregnant woman pose a threat to the fetus and to the mother alike, knowing that a number of drugs approved to treat epileptic seizures increase the risk of the fetus having congenital malformations. Therefore, if a woman has epilepsy and wants to become pregnant, she should consult with the attending physician. Most women with epilepsy are able to get pregnant and give birth to healthy children, but they may be required to change the therapeutic dose of drugs and have to undergo constant and close monitoring and control throughout pregnancy. It is very important to coordinate and consult with the attending physician about planning pregnancy. The emergence of complications that are dangerous to life is not a common occurrence in this disease, but the risks exist. People who have severe, persistent and prolonged seizures are at constant risk of permanent brain damage and even death risk. People who suffer from epileptic seizures, especially those who are not subject to permanent monitoring, may die suddenly and idiopathically, as a result of an epileptic seizure. The risk of sudden and idiopathic death as a result of an epileptic seizure increases if: Epileptic seizures have started at a very early age Epileptic seizures involving more than one part of the brain. Epileptic seizures persist despite taking drug therapy.
