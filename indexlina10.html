What is Asthma

Asthma ("crisis" - Asthma) is a chronic disease that affects humans as a result of inflammation of the airways in the lungs (bronchi - bronchi) and their constriction, which reduces or prevents air flow to these people, causing frequent episodes of dyspnea accompanied by wheezing in the chest area And some other symptoms. As the muscle that surrounds the bronchi contracts and accumulates a large amount of phlegm in the airways leading to blockage, and accordingly ranges symptoms of asthma between crackle and light wheezing when breathing and asthma attacks may endanger life, knowing that children are more affected by the disease. There is no cure for asthma, but its symptoms can be controlled in different ways. Asthma control methods: Avoid stimuli and monitor symptoms Take medications for a long time, and consistently, to prevent asthma attacks Short-term medications to treat asthma in emergency situations, if they arise If asthma is not under observation and follow-up, it may cause frequent and prolonged absenteeism from school or from work which may reduce productivity. The severity of asthma changes with time for most people, so it is necessary to undergo constant health monitoring and follow-up, monitor signs and symptoms and the appropriateness of asthma treatment as needed.

Symptoms of Asthma

Asthma symptoms range from mild to severe, and vary from person to person. Mild symptoms, such as wheezing and rattles, may appear during breathing, asthma attacks may occur from time to time, and asthma symptoms may appear at night hours mainly or only when a physical effort is made. Between seizures, the patient may be in good condition and not face any respiratory difficulties. Asthma symptoms and signs include: Shortness of breath Contractions or chest pain Sleep problems due to shortness of breath Coughing, wheezing or crackling when breathing A beep or crackle sound that can be heard when you breathe out Coughing or cracking attacks during breathing, which are exacerbated by infection with the airways, in cold and flu cases, for example. Signs of worsening of asthma: Exacerbation of symptoms: an increase in the severity and frequency of symptoms of the disease Falling maximum air flow rates: These are measured by the maximum expiratory speed scale, which is a simple device designed to measure the level of lung performance An increased need to use bronchodilators (bronchodilator) - drugs that open the airways, by relaxing the surrounding muscles. It is necessary that the asthma patient is constantly under medical observation, to check whether there is a need to increase the dose and timing of its increase, or to take other measures to treat symptoms or the severity of the disease, until it is brought under control again. If asthma remains to be exacerbated, there may be a need sometimes to go to the hospital. The doctor can help diagnose the signs and symptoms that require going to the hospital emergency room, so that the patient is aware of the cases in which he or she must go to get help.

Causes and risk factors for asthma

It is not clear why some people develop asthma while others do not. Asthma is more likely to be believed to be the result of a mixture of several environmental and genetic factors. The factors that trigger asthma vary from person to person. Exposure to a large number of allergens (allergen - a substance that causes hypersensitivity - Allergen) can provoke signs and symptoms of asthma, including: Airborne allergens, such as: pollen (flower pollen), animal scales, mold, dust mites and cockroaches Airway pollution, such as in common colds Physical activity (exercise-induced asthma) cold air Air pollutants and allergens, such as smoke Certain medications, including beta blockers, aspirin, and other anti-inflammatory drugs do not contain steroids Intense emotion and tension Sulfate (sulfuric acid salts), preservatives added to some food products GERD - Gastroesophageal reflux disease, a condition in which acids return from the stomach and reach the throat Some menstrual periods An allergic reaction to foods, such as pistachios or mollusks. Asthma is a widespread disease and affects millions of adults and children. It is also diagnosed annually with increasing numbers of people, but the cause is still unknown. Asthma risk factors There are a number of factors that are known to increase the risk of asthma, including: Previous cases of asthma in the family Frequent infections of the airway in childhood Passive smoking Life in a civilian area, especially if the air pollution is large Exposure to factors that cause disease in the workplace, such as chemical substances in factories, used in agriculture or hairdressing Small birth weight or obesity.

Complications of asthma

Asthma can cause many complications, including: Go to the emergency room and hospital (lying in the hospital) for acute asthma Permanent narrowing of the airways (restructuring the airways) Side effects due to the prolonged use of certain medications to treat severe asthma.
