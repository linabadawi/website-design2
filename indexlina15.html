What is Oral cancer and precancerous lesions

                        Oral cancer and pharynx are ranked sixth in the place of cancerous diseases in the body.

                        This type of cancer mainly affects, but not only, those over the age of 40. Only 4 out of 100
                        people with multiple cancers have oral cavity cancer (4%).

                        Most of the oral cancers (more than 90%) are from the squamous cell carcinoma in the mucous
                        membrane of the mouth. Also, despite the effort made for early diagnosis and treatment, 50% of
                        people with this cancer eventually die from the disease.

                        It is within the framework of the effort exerted by the medical community to control the spread
                        of the disease, there are efforts made to reduce exposure to carcinogens (abstaining from
                        alcohol and smoking) and to achieve treatment and follow-up for cases that may turn into
                        malignant.

                        One of the goals of permanent follow-up, in particular, is early attempts to diagnose all
                        cancerous or carcinogenic spots, in order to increase the patient's chances of survival. It has
                        also been shown that the patient's chances of survival double if the diagnosis and treatment
                        were made in the initial stages of developing oral cancer. The smaller the time period between
                        the appearance of symptoms that raise suspicions of a cancerous disease in the oral cavity, and
                        until the final diagnosis, the chances of diagnosing the disease in its early cancerous stages
                        increase.

Symptoms of Oral cancer and precancerous lesions

 Oral cancer can present with many symptoms, but the most common symptoms of oral cancer are:
                        1. The wounds present in the mouth are not cured after more than 10 days, especially the absence
                        of a causative agent for these wounds, not even after the presumed cause of wounds has been
                        isolated.

                        2. Local swelling with or without a wound.

                        3. An abnormal white spot of the color of the surrounding mucosa.

                        4. A red spot irregular to the color of the mucous membrane surrounding it.

                        5. Red and white mixed spot.

                        6. Every possible combination of the results shown in sections 1-5

                        7. A brown and even black spot that may be associated with a local swelling or a local wound,
                        but not always.

Causes and risk factors for Parkinson's disease

 It turns out, according to research on this disease, that there is a link between different
                        possible factors and the potential for oral cancer. Among the most important causes of oral
                        cancer that we should mention:

                        1- Smoking and alcohol
                        Especially the consumption of tobacco in every way, especially smoking or chewing, as well as
                        excessive consumption of alcoholic beverages, and in quantities much larger than the amounts
                        used in social events.

                        Today's estimates are that smoking cigarettes more than quadruples the risk of oral cavity
                        cancer compared to people who do not smoke.

                        It was also found that the higher the daily amount of tobacco consumption and the longer the
                        period of tobacco consumption, the greater the risk of oral cavity cancer. Research has shown
                        that a person who smokes one pack of cigarettes a day is more than twice as likely to develop
                        oral cavity cancer than a person who smokes half a packet. Excessive alcohol consumption may
                        increase the risk of infection by tenfold. The combination of smoking and alcohol consumption
                        doubles much more. Prospects for developing oral cavity cancer.

                        2- Certain diseases
                        Also, certain diseases that occur in the oral cavity, such as the lichen planus in the mouth
                        (Oral lichen planus) would be a factor in increasing the risk of developing oral cavity cancer,
                        so it has been shown that 1-2% of people with lichen planus have developed over time. Squamous
                        cell carcinoma in the oral cavity, and decreased immunity in the body as a result of multiple
                        diseases, including AIDS.

                        3- Some medications
                        Taking medications can be a common cause of cancer, including oral cavity cancer.
