What is sclerosis

MS - Multiple sclerosis is a disease that often leads to exhaustion, as the body's immune system damages the membrane surrounding the nerves, and its function is to protect it. This damage or erosion of the membrane adversely affects the communication process between the brain and the rest of the body. Ultimately, the nerves themselves may be damaged, which is beyond repair. Symptoms of sclerosis are different and varied, depending on the affected nerves and the severity of the injury. In severe cases, MS patients lose the ability to walk or speak. Sometimes, it is difficult to diagnose the disease in its early stages, because symptoms often appear and then disappear, and may disappear for several months. Multiple sclerosis (MS) may appear at any age, but it usually begins to develop between the ages of 20 - 40 years. The disease also affects women more than men.

Symptoms of sclerosis

Multiple sclerosis has various symptoms, related to the location of the affected nerve fibers. Among the symptoms of sclerosis:  Numbness (lack of sensation and feeling) or weakness in the extremities, in whole or in part, and this weakness or paralysis usually appears on one side of the body, or in the lower part of it. Generally, partial or complete loss of eyesight in each of the eyes separately, in general (the problem is not in both eyes together at the same time), and sometimes it is accompanied by eye aches when moving them (optic neuritis).  Double or blurry vision. Pain and itching in different parts of the body.  Feeling like an electrical strike as the head moves certain movements.  Tremor, loss of coordination between body parts or loss of balance while walking. fatigue. Dizziness. Symptoms appear in most MS patients, especially in its early stages, and then disappear entirely or partially. Often the symptoms of sclerosis appear or worsen when the body temperature rises.

Causes and risk factors for sclerosis

Sclerosis (MS) is an autoimmune disease (Autoimmune), in which the immune system attacks itself. This process damages the myelin layer, the fatty substance that envelops and protects nerve fibers in the brain and spine. The function of myelin is similar to that of insulating paint in an electrical wire. When the myelin layer is damaged, the message or information that is transmitted through the nerve, it may arrive slower or may not arrive at all. To date, doctors and researchers do not know the exact cause of someone with MS, but not another. But it is known that a mixture of genetic factors and pollution in childhood helps in this. Risk factors The following factors may increase the risk of developing MS: Age: Sclerosis may appear at all ages, but it generally begins to develop and develop, between the ages of 20 - 40 years. Gender: Women are twice as likely to develop MS as men. Genetic factors: The risk of developing MS is increased when there are family members who have, or have had, MS. For example, the risk of developing MS during a person who suffers (or has suffered) from one of his parents or siblings from the disease is 1% - 3%, compared to only 1% in other people. However, trials between identical twins proved that heredity is not the only factor for MS. If multiple sclerosis relates only to genetic factors, the likelihood of infection in identical twins would be equal. However, this is not the case, as the likelihood of infection with identical twins is only 30% if his twin brother has multiple sclerosis. Infections: Many viruses are known to be associated with MS. More recently, the most interesting topic is the relationship between MS and the EB-epstein barr virus - the virus that causes Infectious mononucleosis (also known as Kissing Disease). ). To date, it is not known how the virus originates and develops in difficult cases of MS. Other diseases - people are more (slightly more) at risk of developing multiple sclerosis if they have one of the following autoimmune diseases: Diseases in which the thyroid gland is impaired Type 1 diabetes Inflammatory bowel.

sclerosis complications

In some cases, patients with MS may develop other diseases such as: Hardness or muscle spasms (Myospasm) Paralysis, especially in the legs Problems with the bladder bag, intestine, or sexual performance Mental problems, such as forgetfulness, difficulty concentrating, or depression Epilepsy.
