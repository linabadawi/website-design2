What is Influenza

Influenza is a viral disease that is characterized by a widespread seasonal outbreak. The cause of the virus is the influenza virus, which is transmitted from person to person through the respiratory system.

Flu features

The flu has a number of unique features that include:

  • Stable seasonality: influenza virus causes influenza in winter in the northern hemisphere, in summer in the southern hemisphere and in rainy seasons in the tropics.
  • The ability to change: The influenza virus changes, at a high rate, the structure of proteins on its surface, deceiving the immune system that had produced antibodies against the previous formula. Thus, the disease can affect the person himself more than once.
  • The spread of the disease on a large scale: When the flu spreads, it affects a very large number of children and adults (about a third of children and 10% of adults), which leads to frequent absences from work and school, and creates severe pressure on hospitals and clinics.
The incubation period for incoming disease is shorter (1-4 days). After the incubation period, the following symptoms begin: headache, chills, cough, and later: high temperature, mucus and muscle aches. The high temperature lasts between 3-6 days, but the cough accompanied by sputum can last up to two weeks. People with the flu can transmit the infection in a period of one to two days before the temperature rises and up to 5 days after it has risen. Signs of influenza and the symptoms that accompany it cause distress and discomfort, but they go away and disappear on their own after a few days. However, the problem of influenza is mainly related to its possible complications which include: pneumonitis (or: pneumonitis), bronchitis (or: bronchitis), ear infections and complications in the nervous system. Certain groups are more likely to have complications from influenza. They include: the elderly, patients with chronic respiratory diseases (including Asthma), heart patients, kidney patients, diabetics, patients with congenital or acquired immune deficiency, who are treated with steroid Or by chemotherapy, pregnant women and newborn babies. These groups suffer from a large increase in the rate of complications of influenza and a rise in the rates of hospitalization and death due to illness and its complications. Therefore, it is necessary to prevent or minimize the effects of influenza in members of these groups.

Symptoms of Influenza

Influenza appears at its beginnings as colds and stuffy nose, sneezing and sore throat. But the common cold usually develops slowly, while influenza appears and develops suddenly. While the common cold can be just a passing nuisance, the flu creates a very general bad feeling.

      Common symptoms of influenza include:
  • High body temperature above 38 degrees in adults and between 39.5 and 40 degrees in children and boys
  • Chills and sweats
  • a headache
  • dry cough
  • Muscle pain, especially in the back, arms and legs
  • Feeling of general weakness and fatigue
  • Stuffy nose
  • Anorexia
  • Diarrhea and vomiting in children

Causes and risk factors for Influenza

Influenza viruses circulate in the air inside very small droplets, from coughing, sneezing or even talking to someone who is sick with the flu. These drops can be inhaled directly from the air, or they can be touched through a purpose, such as a phone, a computer keyboard, the computer itself or others, and then transferred to the eyes, nose or mouth. The influenza is divided into three groups (classes) of viruses: A, B and C:

  • Influenza A - can be the leading cause of deadly epidemics on a global scale, striking every 10 to 40 years.
  • Influenza B - causes a milder and limited outbreak. Influenza A and B viruses can be, together or separately, the cause of the spread of influenza, which appears every winter. This, while no relationship was found between the influenza type C virus and the emergence of the influenza pandemic.
  • Influenza C - is a relatively stable virus, while A and B viruses undergo permanent changes, as new varieties always appear. The body produces antibodies against the type of influenza it has contracted, but these antibodies do not prevent infection from the influenza virus of another type. Therefore, doctors recommend influenza vaccination, annually.

    • At-risk group

      The most vulnerable groups with influenza are:
    • Infants and young children
    • People over the age of 50
    • People who live in housing for the elderly or in nursing homes for a long time
    • People with chronic disease such as diabetes, heart disease, kidneys, or lungs
    • People with weakened immune systems, as a result of taking medications or because of HIV
    • Pregnant women during the flu season
    • Workers in medical and treatment facilities or elsewhere where they are exposed to the influenza virus
    • Persons in close and permanent contact with infants or children
    Children who receive long-term aspirin therapy may be more vulnerable to the influenza virus.

Flu complications

If you are generally healthy, influenza infection will not pose a serious risk to your health. Although you are likely to feel very uncomfortable and upset during this, the flu usually resolves without leaving long-term effects.  However, children and the elderly, who are at relatively high risk, may experience complications, such as:

  •  Ear infections
  • Acute sinus infections
  • Bronchitis (bronchitis)
  • Pneumonia
  • Meningitis
Among the aforementioned complications, pneumococci, a serious bacterial infection in the lungs, is the most common and serious complication, to the point that it can be fatal for the elderly and those with chronic diseases. Therefore, vaccination against pneumonia and against the influenza virus provides optimal protection.
