What is Diabetic retinopathy

Diabetic retinopathy is a complication of the small blood vessels, and it is a feature of both types 1 and 2 diabetes. The prevalence of diabetic retinopathy is associated with the period of diabetes. After about 20 years of diabetes, we find that almost all (almost) people with type 1 diabetes, and about 60% of people with type 2 diabetes, have retinopathy, to some degree. Diabetic retinopathy is dangerous for vision. It was possible to link about 86% of cases of blindness, in patients with type 1 diabetes, with diabetic retinopathy, while the proportion of people with type 2 diabetes, who spread among other eye diseases, was approximately one third. Diabetic retinopathy is generally the most common cause of new cases of blindness in the 24- to 47-year-old generation. Diabetic retinopathy, which poses a risk of vision, almost does not appear in patients with type 1 diabetes during the first three to five years of diabetes, even if before the teenage generation. During the next two decades of infection, almost all people with type 1 diabetes develop diabetic retinopathy to some degree. It has recently been found that about 20% of people with type 2 diabetes develop diabetic retinopathy during the initial diagnosis of diabetes, while the disease appears to some degree in most of them in the following decades of the disease. The onset of diabetic retinopathy is accompanied by an advanced blockage of the blood vessels, and then the penetration of the walls of the blood vessels to its circumference, followed by the growth of cells and the emergence of new blood vessels, in the retina and on the posterior surface of the vitreous body. Pregnancy, adolescent generation and unsuccessful surgery may precipitate these changes. Diabetic retinopathy is a product of a combination of systems. Central vision disturbances may be caused by macular edema in the yellow area on the retina (the area of ​​vision in the retina). The new blood vessels can bleed, adding other complications to the vitreous, or even cracking and detachment of the retina.

Treatment of diabetic retinopathy

There are treatment methods that can prevent or hinder the occurrence of retinopathy in diabetics, as well as prevent vision loss in a large number of diabetics. Intensive diabetes treatment, aimed at achieving a good level of control of blood sugar levels, can prevent or hinder the progression and development of diabetic retinopathy. Also, the treatment of early photocoagulation can prevent vision loss in a large number of patients. In some cases, there are no symptoms in those who suffer from diseases that pose a danger to their sense of vision, and therefore it is very important to undergo periodic tests, in order to diagnose retinopathy if it occurs. Eye base imaging is more of a screening test with a higher risk of retinopathy than regular screening. This is, although regular tests are better, in order to detect retinal enlargement associated with molecular tumors, and it is possible to diagnose the growth of new and accurate blood vessels in the region of the optic nerve head, or in other places on the retina. Tips for an initial medical evaluation at an ophthalmologist, or for monitoring diabetes patients: It is desirable that patients with type 1 diabetes undergo a comprehensive eye examination, with pupillary expansion, by an ophthalmologist or optometrist, after 3 to 5 years of diabetes. Likewise, patients with type 2 diabetes must undergo a comprehensive initial eye examination, at an ophthalmologist or optometrist, shortly after they are diagnosed. Patients with type 1 and type 2 diabetes must return to the eye examination every year, at an ophthalmologist or optometrist, with knowledge and experience in diagnosing diabetic retinopathy. If the condition is advanced, then the period between the examination and the other should be shorter. Women with diabetes who wish to become pregnant and give birth should undergo a comprehensive eye examination, and receive advice about the risks of developing or worsening diabetic retinopathy. Likewise, pregnant women with diabetes must perform a comprehensive eye examination during the first trimester of pregnancy, and continue to monitor and follow up their condition throughout all months of pregnancy. Patients with diabetic retinopathy, of any degree, need immediate treatment by an experienced and knowledgeable ophthalmologist in diabetic retinopathy. Treating diabetic retinopathy with laser at this stage can help 50% reduce the risk of acute vision loss. Patients who have lost vision due to diabetes need vision restoration and treatment, with the assistance of an ophthalmologist or optometrist, with the appropriate rehabilitation and experience in the field of low vision.
