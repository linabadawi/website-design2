What is a strokes

Stroke, formerly known in English as a cerebrovascular accident - CVA, occurs when the blood flow to one part of the brain stops or is severely obstructed, depriving the brain tissue of the much needed oxygen and other vital nutrients. As a result, the brain cells die within a few minutes. Stroke is a medical emergency, and immediate treatment is very vital and important, as it can reduce the damage to the brain and prevent possible complications after the stroke. Fortunately, stroke can be treated. Raising control over most stroke risk factors, such as Hypertension, smoking and High blood cholesterol, is likely the main cause of decreased stroke cases.

Symptoms of stroke

Attention should be paid to the following early signs: Difficulty walking: if a person suffers a stroke, he may falter, feel dizzy, lose his balance or lose coordination ability (between the senses, movement and speech)  Difficulties in speaking: If a person suffers a stroke, his speech may become dull or lose the ability to find appropriate words to describe what is happening to him and him (aphasia, or loss of language - Aphasia). Try repeating a simple sentence. If you can't do it, you will likely have a stroke.  Numbness on one side of the body: If a person suffers a stroke, he or she may lose sensation, or feel a paraplegia (paralysis on one side of the body). Try to lift both arms of your arms above your head at the same time. If you start one of them, you may have a stroke. Vision difficulties: If a person suffers a stroke, he or she may experience blurred vision suddenly, he may lose vision for a few moments, or he may suffer from intercourse (double vision, or: double vision - Diplopia) Headache: headache that appears suddenly and without warning, or unusual headache, which may be accompanied by cramping in the neck, facial pain, pain in the eyes, sudden vomiting or changes in the cognitive state - it may indicate, at times, a stroke . For most people, the first sign of a possible stroke is a transient ischemic attack. A transient ischemic attack is a temporary defect in the delivery of blood to one part of the brain. Symptoms of a transient ischemic attack are the same as stroke symptoms, but they last for a shorter period of time - from a few minutes to 24 hours, and then fade and go away without leaving any lasting damage. A person may have more than one transient ischemic attack, and the signs and symptoms associated with each may be the same or different. The occurrence of a transient ischemic attack in a particular person may indicate that this person is at risk of developing a severe stroke. Also, those who have experienced a transient ischemic attack are more likely to have a stroke than those who have not suffered before.

Causes and risk factors for stroke

A stroke occurs if there is a problem or an imbalance in the amount of blood coming to the brain. The most common type of stroke - ischemic stroke - is the delivery of a small amount of blood to the brain. The other type of stroke - hemorrhagic stroke - is an excess of blood in the skull.

Stroke complications

Depending on how long the brain has suffered from a lack of blood supply, a stroke can cause a variety of disabilities that may be temporary, or may be permanent. The possible complications due to stroke vary with the affected part of the brain. These complications include: Paralysis or loss of ability to move muscles Difficulty speaking or swallowing Memory loss or general comprehension problems Aches People who develop a stroke, sometimes, become introverted and less mixed and involved in social life. They may lose the ability to take care of themselves, and may need nursing care to assist them in daily tasks, such as personal hygiene and others.  As in most cases in which the brain suffers any damage, so are the complications of stroke, as the success of its treatment varies from person to person.
